The mathematical model

From Newton's second law to the governing second-order ODE and its characteristic equation, how our derivation lines up with the original paper, and the four damping regimes the discriminant produces.

The three components

A mass-spring-damper system has three components: a mass suspended over a spring and a damper. The spring provides a restoring force, the damper removes energy, and the mass carries the inertia. The interaction of the three determines how the system returns to equilibrium.

Mass

The body of mass \(m\), measured in kilograms. Its inertia resists changes in velocity, giving the term \(m\ddot{y}\).

Damping coefficient

The constant \(c\), measured in \(\mathrm{N\,s/m}\). It sets the strength of the damping force \(-c\dot{y}\) that opposes motion.

Spring constant

The stiffness \(k\), measured in \(\mathrm{N/m}\). By Hooke's law it gives the restoring force \(-ky\).

Deriving the equation of motion

The displacement \(y(t)\) of the body is governed by Newton's second law, which equates the inertial term to the resultant force.

\( m\,\ddot{y} = \sum F \)

At static equilibrium the spring stretch \(\delta\) makes the spring force balance gravity, \(-k\delta + mg = 0\), so when displacement is measured from equilibrium those constant terms cancel and play no part in the motion. The two forces that remain are the restoring force and the damping force.

\( F_{\text{spring}} = -k\,y \qquad F_{\text{damping}} = -c\,\dot{y} \)

Summing the forces gives \( m\ddot{y} = -ky - c\dot{y} \), which rearranges into the governing second-order homogeneous linear ODE with constant coefficients.

\( m\,\ddot{y} + c\,\dot{y} + k\,y = 0 \)

Substituting the trial solution \( y = e^{\lambda t} \) and dividing by \(m\) gives the characteristic equation, whose roots decide the type of motion.

\( \lambda^2 + \dfrac{c}{m}\lambda + \dfrac{k}{m} = 0 \)

For numerical work the equation is reduced to a system of two first-order equations, with \( y_1 = y \) and \( y_2 = \dot{y} \).

\( \dot{y}_1 = y_2, \qquad \dot{y}_2 = \dfrac{-c\,y_2 - k\,y_1}{m} \)

Points we corrected in the paper

The critical-damping solution

For \(c = 8\), the paper prints the solution as \((1+4)e^{-4x}\), which drops the variable and collapses to a constant times an exponential. The correct repeated-root solution is \((1 + 4t)e^{-4t}\), and that is what our graphs use. The linear-in-\(t\) factor is exactly what marks critical damping.

Labelling of the roots

In the critical and overdamped cases the paper labels the roots "complex conjugate", but for \(c = 8\) they are a repeated real root \(\lambda = -4\), and for \(c = 10\) they are distinct real roots \(\lambda = -2, -8\). Only the underdamped case has genuinely complex roots.

The Van der Pol naming

The paper's MATLAB routine is named for the Van der Pol oscillator, which is nonlinear with amplitude-dependent damping. Our system is linear with constant coefficients, so although the code integrates the correct linear system, the name is a carry-over rather than a description of the physics.

What stays the same

None of these points change the conclusions. With \(m = 1\), \(k = 16\) and \(c = 0, 2, 8, 10\), our solutions and graphs agree with the paper case by case, which is the heart of the replication on the analysis page.

The four damping regimes

Writing the discriminant as \( \Delta = c^2 - 4mk \), its sign sorts the motion into four cases. The roots are \( \lambda_{1,2} = -\alpha \pm \beta \) with \( \alpha = \tfrac{c}{2m} \) and \( \beta = \tfrac{1}{2m}\sqrt{c^2 - 4mk} \).

Undamped

\(c = 0\). No energy loss, so the mass oscillates forever at its natural frequency.

\( y = A\cos\omega_0 t + B\sin\omega_0 t \)

Underdamped

\( \Delta < 0 \). Complex roots give a decaying oscillation at the damped frequency.

\( y = Ce^{-\alpha t}\cos(\omega^{*} t - \delta) \)

Critically damped

\( \Delta = 0 \). A repeated real root gives the fastest return to rest with no oscillation.

\( y = (c_1 + c_2 t)\,e^{-\alpha t} \)

Overdamped

\( \Delta > 0 \). Two distinct real roots; the mass creeps back to rest without crossing it.

\( y = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t} \)
