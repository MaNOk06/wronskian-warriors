Problem definition

The mass-spring-damper system is one of the most important models in engineering and physics, used to analyse how objects respond to forces. It lets engineers understand oscillations, so they can predict how structures will move, absorb energy, and respond to external events. Understanding how things behave when they vibrate is what makes it possible to design safer vehicle suspensions, rotating machinery, and sensitive instruments.

The fundamental concepts of the model are applied across engineering to solve problems in vibration control, from designing skyscrapers that withstand dynamic forces like strong wind, to robotics that achieve more stable movement. The same model is used to study the response of many dynamic systems.

The system has three main components: the spring, the damper, and the mass suspended over them. The spring has a stiffness \(k\) and a restoring force. The mass \(m\) produces an inertial force, the internal resistance due to mass and acceleration. The damper, with damping coefficient \(c\), modifies the system's natural oscillation, and the amount of damping relative to the mass and stiffness dictates how the system returns to its equilibrium position.

Assumptions made in the model

  1. The mass of the spring is negligible compared with the body.
  2. Motion is strictly vertical, with no horizontal or rotational movement.
  3. The parameters \(m\), \(c\) and \(k\) are constants and do not change with time.
  4. Downward displacement is taken as positive and upward as negative.

Deriving the equation of motion

Consider a spring suspended vertically from a fixed support, with a body of mass \(m\) attached to the bottom end. When the body is pulled down a certain distance and released, it oscillates vertically. The displacement \(y\) is a function of time. The motion is governed by Newton's second law, which states that an object's acceleration depends on its mass and the resultant force acting on it.

\( \sum F = m\,a = m\,\ddot{y} \)

The damping force opposes motion due to energy loss, gradually bringing the oscillation to a stop.

\( F_{\text{damping}} = -c\,\dot{y} \)

The spring is initially unstretched. When the body is attached, gravity \(mg\) stretches it until the spring force balances the gravitational force, leaving the system at its equilibrium position. Displacement \(y\) is measured from this equilibrium.

\( k\,\delta = m g \)

If the body is displaced down from equilibrium, the spring stretches further. By Hooke's law the restoring force is proportional to the displacement, pulling the body back toward \(y = 0\).

\( F_{\text{spring}} = -k\,y \)

Summing all the forces acting on the system through Newton's second law gives the final ordinary differential equation for the damped mass-spring system.

\( m\,\ddot{y} + c\,\dot{y} + k\,y = 0 \)

where \(m\) is the mass, \(c\) is the damping coefficient and \(k\) is the spring constant, with initial conditions

\( y(0) = y_0, \qquad \dot{y}(0) = v_0 \)

Some applications

Vehicle suspension

Keeps a car steady and comfortable over bumps in the road.

Skyscrapers

Helps tall buildings resist strong wind and other dynamic forces.

Rotating machinery

Controls vibration in engines, motors and spinning parts.

Robotics

Gives robots steadier, more controlled movement.
